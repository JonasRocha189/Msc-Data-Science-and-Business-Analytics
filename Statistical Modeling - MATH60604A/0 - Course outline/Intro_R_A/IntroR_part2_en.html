Datasets used

Electricity data

Weâll illustrate the use of different concepts and commands in R using a dataset on household electricity consumption between January 1991 and December 2000. Each line of the dataset represents information for one given month. The data are saved in the electricbill.txt and electricbill.csv files. The dataset includes the following variables:

  • NUM: identification of the observation
  • YEAR: year
  • MONTH: month
  • BILL: bill amount, in dollars (including 5% sale tax)
  • TEMP: mean temperature (in Fahrenheit)
  • HDD: measure quantifying the amount of energy necessary to heat the building
  • CDD: measure quantifying the amount of energy necessary to cool the building
  • SIZE: number of people living in the house
  • METER: presence of a new electrical meter (1=yes, 0=no)
  • PUMP1: presence of a new heat pump (1=yes, 0=no)
  • PUMP2: presence of a new heat pump for the second time (1=yes, 0=no)
  • RIDER TOTAL: total load (by kwh)
  • CONSUMPTION: consumption (in kwh)

Baby-boom data

You will practice the concepts learned using the babyboom.txt dataset. The data contains information on the time of birth, sex and birth weight of 44 babies born in a 24 hour period at the Brisbane Hospital, Australia. The variables are as follows:

  • Time: time of birth
  • Sex: babyâs sex (1=female, 2=male)
  • Weight: babyâs birthweight (in grams)
  • Time.midnight: number of minutes past midnight at the moment of birth

Graphs

R can produce a wide variety of high quality graphs in various formats (wmf, ps, jpg, pdf, png, bmp). There are R libraries such as ggplot and ggplot2 that allow you to create graphics and figures of exceptional quality. The syntax of these functions is not necessarily obvious to a beginner in R, and we will not go into detail here.

Here is basic code for using the plot function in R; this allows to create a simple scatterplot:

plot(mydata$BILL,mydata$CONSUMPTION)

We can easily add a title to the graph, and label the axes:

plot(mydata$BILL,mydata$CONSUMPTION, main="Scatter plot of bill vs consumption",
     xlab="Bill", ylab="Consumption")

We can also add color and use different symbols for the pointsâ¦

plot(mydata$BILL,mydata$CONSUMPTION, main="Scatter plot of bill vs consumption",
     xlab="Bill", ylab="Consumption",pch=21, bg="red")

The following code shows how to add the best fitting line passing through the points, as well as vertical and horizontal lines:

plot(mydata$BILL,mydata$CONSUMPTION, main="Scatter plot of bill vs consumption",
        xlab="Bill", ylab="Consumption")
regress=lm(mydata$CONSUMPTION~mydata$BILL)
abline(regress)
abline(h=6000, col="green")
abline(v=100, col="red")

Here is another example, where the points on the graph are connected by a line thereby allowing to visualize the trend or trajectory:

plot(mydata$NUM,mydata$BILL,type="l",xlab="Time",ylab="Bill")

We can also produce histograms:

hist(mydata$BILL, main = "Bill", xlab = "Range", ylab="Frequency")

And boxplots:

# single boxplot
boxplot(mydata$BILL,main="Bill")

# boxplots side by side
boxplot(mydata$BILL~mydata$MONTH,main="Bill")

We can also group several graphs together, so that they are displayed side by side:

par(mfrow=c(1,2))
plot(mydata$BILL,mydata$CONSUMPTION,xlab="Bill", ylab="Consumption")
plot(mydata$TEMP,mydata$CONSUMPTION,xlab="Temp", ylab="Consumption")

par(mfrow=c(1,1))
